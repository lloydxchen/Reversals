## A. Setup types & names he uses

- **Delta Tail (buy tail)**: On a delta footprint chart, the bar shows a single isolated level of negative delta at the bottom of a green (up-closing) bar, while all other price levels in the bar show positive delta. The "tail" is that lone negative delta level at the bottom. Bullish setup — signals exhaustion of sellers and directional confirmation. (v157, "Delta Footprints", [05:15])

- **Delta Tail (sell tail)**: Mirror image — on a red (down-closing) bar, a single isolated positive delta level sits at the top, while all other price levels show negative delta. Bearish setup. (v157, "Delta Footprints", [05:44])

- **Stacked imbalance in context — post-breakout continuation**: Stacked buying imbalance that appears *immediately after* a new high has been made (not as price is approaching the high). Mike explicitly prefers this variant over stacked imbalances that print as price probes up to a high. Bullish continuation. (v159, "Weekly Group Training", [08:31])

- **Stacked imbalance reversal at extreme**: Stack selling imbalance appearing *at the high of day* off a prior swing high, used as a reversal signal. (v159, "Weekly Group Training", [12:38])

- **Prominent POC (fuchsia) at extreme**: Prominent point of control appearing at swing high as bearish confirmation, or swing low as bullish. Used as a filter / confirming signal rather than standalone. (v159, "Weekly Group Training", [03:05]; v160, "Turns", [13:02])

- **Delta divergence at extreme**: Negative delta printing at a new high (bearish divergence); positive delta printing at a new low (bullish divergence). Used with Price Rejector / Turns indicator signals. (v159, "Weekly Group Training", [07:55]; v160, "Turns", [19:05])

- **Orderflows Turns signal**: Proprietary multi-factor indicator analyzing delta, POC, overall volume (bid vs ask), price action, and swing analysis to generate buy/sell "turn" signals. Each signal marks a potential area of price defense/support. (v160, "Turns", [00:53])

- **Multiple imbalances in a bar (off low)**: Three or more imbalances in a single bar that are not necessarily stacked on adjacent price levels; appears at the low as an early sign of buying aggression. Bullish. Used preferentially over stacked imbalances when spotting the low. (v159, "Weekly Group Training", [03:35])

- **"Terrific low-risk trading opportunity"**: Highest-grade language in v157 — applied specifically to a delta sell-tail at the high of day WITH divergence AND ratio signal in the next bar. The combination of three signals (tail + divergence + ratio) elevates to this tier. (v157, [13:34])

- **"Decent trade"**: Applied to a buy delta tail at a moderate level (not HOD/LOD), with stacked imbalances as additional confirmation. Moderate tier. (v157, [06:53])

- **"Borderline"**: Applied to a crude oil delta tail example where the bar had an isolated volume level in addition to the tail, creating ambiguity. (v157, [19:40])

- **"Doesn't qualify" / "not qualifying"**: Applied to bars with fewer than a minimum number of price levels, or bars with more than one contrary delta level in the tail area. Explicit disqualifiers used repeatedly. (v157, [11:24], [12:20], [14:39])

- **"I'd be interested" / "I might be interested"**: Applied to stacked imbalance setups coming after a swing low with early positive delta evidence — a tentative, conditional grade. (v159, "Weekly Group Training", [15:43])

- **"Kiss of death"**: Applied to extremely strong negative delta printing suddenly after a series of smaller negative deltas at a high — signals the aggressive sellers have arrived and the market is about to tank. (v159, "Weekly Group Training", [13:59])

- **What moves a delta tail from decent to "terrific" (A+)**:
  1. Location at HOD or LOD — "I love getting signals at highs of the day" (v157, [13:09])
  2. Additional confirming signals in the same or next bar: divergence + ratio (v157, [13:34])
  3. Candle color match — must be a green candle for buy tail, red candle for sell tail (v157, [21:34])
  4. Bar must have sufficient price levels — short bars (3–4 price levels) explicitly disqualified (v157, [12:20])

- **What downgrades a stacked imbalance signal**:
  - Appears in the middle of a consolidation range (not at a clean extreme) — "probably not going to be very effective" (v159, [08:03])
  - Appears after a large directional move already in (e.g., 75-cent rally in crude) — "I'd be thinking the opposite" (v159, [02:42])

  - Lacks follow-up bars with strong confirming delta after the signal bar (v159, [10:55])

## C. Order-flow story & psychology per setup

- **Delta Tail buy**: Sellers made one last aggressive push down to the low of the bar (creating the negative delta tail), but buyers overwhelmed them at every other price level in the bar. The isolated tail seller is exhausted or was a failed probe. Price reverts upward. (v157, [05:15])

- **Delta Tail sell**: Buyers made one last aggressive push up to the high of the bar, but sellers were dominant at every other level. The tail buyer is trapped/exhausted. Price reverts downward. (v157, [05:44])

- **Candle-direction rule for delta tail**: "I'm never looking to buy a red candle... I want price to start moving in the direction I'm trading." The green-candle requirement for a buy tail is a confirmation that the market is already moving in the right direction; buying a red candle with a tail is described as "a recipe for disaster." (v157, [21:34])

- **Late-to-the-party buyers in a stacked imbalance**: After a 75-cent crude rally, a stacked buying imbalance represents late retail buyers finally entering — not smart money — and is a low-quality signal. (v159, [02:18])

- **Stop-triggering psychology at new highs**: When price breaks to a new high and a stacked imbalance appears immediately after, Mike interprets this as stops being triggered above the high providing momentum; the vacuum (empty volume at that level, price not pulling back to fill) confirms the strength. (v159, [11:21])

- **"Kiss of death" delta surge**: When a market is near a high with moderate negative delta, then suddenly a bar prints extremely strong negative delta (e.g., -400 to -700 on crude 8-range), this is the aggressive sellers finally arriving — it confirms the reversal is underway. (v159, [13:59])

- **Multiple imbalances at the low vs. stacked at the high**: Mike wants to see aggressive buying coming *in off the low* (multiple imbalances) as a bullish sign, not aggressive buying appearing after the rally is already complete. Psychology: want smart money defending the low, not retail chasing the high. (v159, [04:06])

## D. Exhaustion clues

- **Delta tail itself**: A single contrary delta level at the extreme of a bar is direct evidence that the last push in that direction was absorbed and overwhelmed. "Inflection points" is his exact term. (v157, [02:14])

- **Alternating single prints**: If single prints alternate (green, red, green, red) at a level, this signals range/chop rather than exhaustion at an extreme — the opposite of a clean exhaustion print. (v159, [17:06])

- **Single print at the end of an up move / swing high**: Single print on a red bar appearing at a swing high is an exhaustion signal; single print on a green bar at the same location is less interesting to him. (v159, [18:23])

- **"Kiss of death" delta**: Suddenly extreme negative delta appearing at a high after a period of moderate negative delta — the exhausted bears are replaced by aggressive sellers confirming the top. (v159, [13:59])

- **Divergence at HOD/LOD**: Negative delta at new price high (bearish exhaustion of buying); divergence signal printed as triangle on software. (v159, [04:00])

## E. Absorption clues

- **Post-breakout stacked imbalance that does not fill back in**: When a stacked buying imbalance fires above a breakout level and price does not pull back to fill that area, Mike reads it as strong absorption of supply — "that's how you know how strong it is." [ONCE] (v159, [11:52])

- **Strong positive delta string after a divergence low**: Multiple bars with consecutive strong positive delta (e.g., delta surge) after a divergence low signal = buyers absorbing selling at the extreme. (v159, [11:21])

- **Prominent POC as absorption magnet**: A large fuchsia prominent POC at the high or low represents concentrated price defense/absorption at that level. If the market runs to it and stalls, absorption is occurring there. (v159, [13:02]; v160, [13:02])

## F. Stacked imbalance ideas

- **Context is the primary filter for stacked imbalances** — the signal quality depends entirely on *where* it fires:
  1. Best: immediately after a new high breakout (buys) / new low breakdown (sells) — stops triggered, vacuum left (v159, [08:31])
  2. Good: at the low of day with additional positive delta evidence (v159, [15:43])
  3. Poor: in the middle of a consolidation range (v159, [08:03])
  4. Poor: after a large pre-existing move in that direction (v159, [02:42])
  5. Poor: as price *probes toward* a high (not after the actual breakout) (v159, [09:01])

- **Follow-up delta after stacked imbalance is mandatory**: After a stack selling imbalance, the very next bar should show a "nice healthy negative Delta not a small Delta." If the follow-up bar has weak delta, the signal is downgraded. [REPEATED] (v159, [10:55])

- **"Near stacking" on the way up**: Multiple bars with 3 imbalances that aren't fully stacked — described as lower quality than a clean stacked signal; leads to skepticism about the breakout. (v159, [02:42])

- **Stack imbalance vacuum**: When stacked buying imbalance prints at a breakout level and price does not return to fill it, confirming strength — likely ~1,000 contracts traded (stop-triggered) without fill. [ONCE] (v159, [11:52])

## G. Delta / delta-divergence ideas

- **Delta footprint (horizontal comparison)**: Takes the raw delta (buy vol − sell vol) at each price level within a bar and compares horizontally across the bar, producing a delta value per row. This is distinct from the bid-ask footprint. Setting: Ladder Content → Sell Tax → change from "Bid-Ask" to "Delta" on Orderflows Trader software. (v157, [03:12])

- **Diagonal delta**: An alternative mode comparing delta diagonally across price levels (108 vs 54 at adjacent rows). Mentioned as a separate presentation topic; not detailed here. [ONCE] (v157, [04:45])

- **Delta tail operationalization (strict rule)**:
  - Buy tail: green candle, exactly one price level with negative delta at the bottom, all other levels positive delta. [REPEATED emphasis on being strict]
  - Sell tail: red candle, exactly one price level with positive delta at the top, all other levels negative delta.
  - Disqualified if: more than one contrary level; bar has too few price levels (3–4 price levels explicitly disqualified); contrary level is in the middle rather than at the extreme. (v157, [06:20], [11:24], [12:20])

- **Delta surge as bullish confirmation**: After a buy signal (divergence + ratio), seeing delta spike strongly positive (55, 326, 406 in successive bars) confirms bulls are in control and stops are being triggered. (v159, [11:21])

- **Monitoring delta intrabar for exit signal**: While in a trade, monitor delta for sign of reversal. A bar with strong contrary delta (e.g., +1,400 positive delta while short) is a signal to exit/scratch. "If you're short and you see strong positive delta 1400 — just get out." [REPEATED concept] (v160, [20:20])

- **Consecutive bars confirming trend delta**: While price is in a directional move, a string of consistently negative (or positive) deltas — even if some are small — can sustain the trade; one strong contrary delta bar is a warning; a few strong contrary delta bars signals the move may be over. (v159, [15:07])

## R. Contradictions / nuances

- **Delta tail strictness vs. practical flexibility**: Mike repeatedly emphasizes being "very strict" on the delta tail (one contrary level only, right bar size, candle color match), yet acknowledges "it might work quite often" when the criteria are looser. This tension is unresolved — the strict definition is presented as the formal rule, but he implies loosening it slightly is workable in practice. (v157, [23:04])

- **Stacked imbalance after a move vs. at a breakout**: The digest notes stacked imbalances are immediate-term S/R only (~5 bars). This chunk adds a refinement: the timing relative to the breakout determines quality. A stacked buying imbalance BEFORE the new high is a probe/fake; AFTER the new high it's a genuine continuation signal. This is a finer distinction than the digest's blanket 5-bar decay note. (v159, [08:31], [09:01])

- **Single print: direction-dependent interpretation**: Single prints at swing highs (on red bars) or swing lows (on green bars) = exhaustion/reversal clue. Single prints on bars mid-move (in the direction of the move) = continuation confirmation. Alternating single prints = chop. This three-way interpretation is not fully captured by the digest's "single print/exhaustion" entry. (v159, [17:06], [20:08])

- **Orderflows Turns look-back = 2 for Euro currency**: The digest lists "look-back filter" as a known NT parameter but does not specify per-market values. Euro currency (6E) uses look-back of 2 vs. standard 7, because 6E is "more volatile" and "gets more price movements" on an 8-range chart. This is a concrete per-instrument parameter. (v160, [04:22])

- **Delta tail candle color = hard rule not soft preference**: Presented as an absolute disqualifier ("I'm never looking to buy a red candle"), not just a preference. The digest's "candle-color agreement" is listed as a discriminator but this chunk makes it a hard binary gate for the delta tail setup specifically. (v157, [21:34])

- **"Works immediately" on delta tail**: While the digest notes "works immediately ≈ within 1–3 bars," this chunk implies the window is even tighter for the delta tail setup on shorter charts — 10 minutes on a 1-minute chart is already too long. On a 4-range chart the implication is within the next 2–3 bars maximum. (v160, [33:34])

- **Thin market volume threshold**: Digest notes "same settings every market" for the detector only. This chunk explicitly states that for thin markets (NQ, YM), the volume threshold in Orderflows Turns is critical and must be adjusted, while for ES it "doesn't matter so much." This is a specific per-instrument parameter nuance. (v160, [39:21])
