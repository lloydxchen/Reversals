## A. Setup types & names he uses

- **Delta Surge** (bullish/bearish): steady increase in directional delta over several bars, taken in context of market location (low or high). A named, discrete setup — one of his two stated "favorite" setups with low risk and high reward potential. (v184, [32:25])
- **Multiple Imbalances** (bullish/bearish): 3 or more buying imbalances in a green/up bar, or 3 or more selling imbalances in a red/down bar; distinct from stacked imbalances (which require neat vertical stacking). Named second "favorite" setup. (v184, [36:20])
- **Delta divergence / cumulative delta divergence** (bearish at HOD): market makes new higher high while cumulative delta is flat or declining — signals weakening and potential reversal. (v183, [32:11])
- **Absorption at swing low** (bullish): aggressive selling dries up (min delta collapses) at a swing low, followed by a large aggressive buying bar; shows sellers have been removed from the market. (v184, [22:35])
- **Max/Min delta internal reading** (context/confirmation/exit): reading max delta, min delta, and final delta within and across bars to gauge conviction of aggressors and signal exhaustion or absorption. (v183, [11:22])

## C. Order-flow story & psychology per setup

- **Delta Surge (bullish)**: Aggressive buyers step up sequentially at a low. The story: market comes off a low, sellers were dominant on the way down (strong negative delta, high min delta). Then sellers begin to exhaust (min delta shrinks, volume normalizes), and buyers show up incrementally stronger bar over bar. The final bar with multiple buying imbalances confirms the buyers have won the tug-of-war. (v184, [22:35] and [33:32])
- **Absorption at swing low**: Sellers come in hard (delta -1300, -699, -723 at the lows), then suddenly only -211, -78, -75 appear — smaller volume tells you the sellers are done. The trapped sellers who sold on the way down are now the fuel: they must buy back as prices rise, turning them into forced buyers. (v184, [22:35] and [23:40])
- **Cumulative delta divergence (bearish)**: Market is going higher on the same or declining cumulative delta. The story: not enough net aggressive buying to justify the higher price. The big players are not participating; the move lacks fuel. When cumulative delta peaks and starts declining while price holds near highs, sellers have stepped in. (v183, [32:11])
- **Failed rally / "point B" trap**: Bullish setup appears (nice green bars, positive delta) but order flow flatlines after entry. The story: the aggressive buying was exhausted in those two bars; no fresh buyers stepped in to continue. Traders who got long are now stranded; if order flow dies, the trade idea is already discounted by the market. (v184, [19:02])
- **Institutional supply absorption**: When a market goes sideways while strong selling (strong min delta) appears but aggressive buyers absorb the selling (max delta rises modestly), price doesn't fall. This is the institutional "pause and distribute" pattern: *"you don't want to put your foot on the gas pedal right you sell some you let the market pause … then you sell some more."* (v183, [39:33])

## E. Absorption clues

- **Absorption signature (three-step)**: (1) strong prior-directional delta fades; (2) three small bars with near-neutral volume appear at the swing low, each with shrinking min delta; (3) a big green bar with strong max delta, multiple buying imbalances, and rising POC confirms absorption is complete. (v184, [22:35]–[24:43]) [REPEATED]
- **POC rising across absorption bars**: *"point of controls are higher one two three"* — even while delta is mixed and candles are small, if POCs are sequentially rising, buyers are defending progressively higher prices. Absorption signal. (v184, [22:35]) [ONCE with this specific detail]
- **Max delta expanding while price stalls**: At a low, if you see max delta growing bar by bar (32, 85, 142 → then 1689, 1748) while price goes sideways, buyers are absorbing the remaining sell flow. The burst bar after is the confirmation. (v183, [25:26]) [REPEATED concept, fresh numbers]

## F. Stacked imbalance ideas

- **Multiple imbalances vs. stacked imbalances — explicit distinction**: Stacked imbalances require imbalances to be "neatly stacked on top of each other" (vertically adjacent price levels). Multiple imbalances (3+) in a bar do NOT need to be stacked — they can be spread out within the bar. The key insight: *"a lot of bars have just one maybe two buying imbalances which doesn't necessarily mean anything but when you have a bar that's more than three three or more preferably that means something."* (v184, [37:28] and [38:57]) [REPEATED]
- **Multiple imbalances = earlier entry vs. stacked**: *"you're going to miss getting in earlier on the street"* — multiple imbalances fires before the stacked imbalance pattern is visible; at the same swing high, the multiple imbalances bar appears before the one tidy stacked imbalance bar. - **At swing highs, selling imbalances within a bar signal the reversal even when not stacked**: The market at swing high showed *"multiple sound imbalances not so later at a lower price"* — implies looking for the first multi-imbalance bar at the turning point, not waiting for the stacked pattern. ## I. Trapped-trader ideas

- **Trapped sellers at a low**: Sellers who dominated on the way down are trapped when buyers absorb the last selling. - **Trapped longs at a high (stall + deteriorating order flow)**: Traders who got long on an apparent bullish setup (point B example) find no follow-through in order flow; the market stalls, delta turns negative.
