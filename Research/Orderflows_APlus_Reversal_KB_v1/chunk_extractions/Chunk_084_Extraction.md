## A. Setup types & names he uses

- **Delta flip** (bearish/bullish): bar starts strong in one direction (max delta), then aggressive opposite-side flow reverses it so the bar closes in the opposite direction to max delta. (v271, "Discover New Trading Strategies", [30:28]–[38:33])
- **Stopping volume** (bearish/bullish): heavy volume concentrated at a price extreme; price level with the highest per-price quantity in the bar, often at HOD/LOD, signaling that large supply or demand is blocking further movement. Bullish = prominent blue POC at a low; bearish = prominent magenta/red POC at a high. (v271, [38:33]–[43:24]; v278, [13:30]–[15:03])
- **Delta pressure** (supportive buying or resistance selling): market moves in one direction while delta is counter-directional because passive traders are absorbing aggressive flow — green bars going up on negative delta (supportive passive buyers absorbing sellers), or red bars going down on positive delta (passive sellers distributing supply against buyers). (v271, [43:55]–[52:37])
- **Multiple imbalances** (bullish/bearish): imbalances spread throughout a bar but not stacked neatly; less visible than stacked imbalances but equally valid signal. (v271, [23:28]–[29:58])
- **Stacked imbalances** (bullish/bearish): neatly clustered contiguous imbalances in a bar. On volatile/event days, stacked imbalances represent large institutional orders needing fast execution. (v271, [23:28]; v275, [04:18]–[05:54])
- **Inverse imbalance** (trapped-trader reversal): stacked selling imbalance at the bottom of a green bar (bullish); stacked buying imbalance at the top of a red bar (bearish) — dark red display in software for bearish, blue for bullish. Signals trapped traders; scalp opportunity in opposite direction. (v275, [07:23]–[08:16]; [14:15]–[15:51])
- **POC-price relationship at swing high/low** (bullish/bearish): monitoring where price closes relative to the bar's point of control to read weakening trend and potential reversal. (v273, [06:07]–[14:20]; v274, [00:00]–[17:52])
- **Ratio + divergence at low** (bullish): mentioned alongside stacked buying imbalance and exhaustion print as a multi-confluence bullish signal at the low of the day. (v274, [06:22]–[07:16])
- **Prominent POC** (bullish/bearish): highlighted POC that appears at the extreme of a bar (top or bottom); software-detected. Bullish = blue; bearish = magenta/red. (v271, [42:03]–[42:56]; v278, [13:30]–[15:03])

## C. Order-flow story & psychology per setup

- **Delta flip**: Aggressive buying drives max delta high in the bar; then large passive sellers (or aggressive sellers) absorb/overwhelm all of that buying and force the close back to negative. "The aggressive selling came in, reversed all of that, and it closed down." The key is that participants who bought the early spike are now trapped offside. (v271, [33:13]–[34:28])
- **Delta pressure — going down on positive delta**: Passive sellers with "deeper pockets" and a "better idea of what's happening" are distributing supply against every aggressive buyer. They don't need to hit bids aggressively because they know buyers will keep appearing; they simply offer out their supply at each uptick. Eventually passive supply exhausts all the buying and price falls. (v271, [47:00]–[49:24])
- **Delta pressure — going up on negative delta**: Supportive passive buyers absorb every wave of aggressive selling until sellers "shot all their bullets, they got nothing left," and then the market "shoots up very quickly." (v271, [45:51]–[47:00])
- **Stopping volume**: A large participant "puts their foot down" — offers or bids an outsized quantity at one price level, preventing further movement. Half the bar's total volume trading at a single price at the extreme is cited as an example. (v271, [40:32])
- **Stacked imbalances on event day**: Result of an institutional execution desk needing to fill a large order immediately ("buy me 750 e-minis" / "sell me 1200 e-minis") with no ability to finesse, otherwise the market moves away from them. (v275, [04:51]–[05:54])
- **POC-price relationship**: On a trending move, price closes beyond (away from) the POC in the direction of trend. When price starts closing on the wrong side of POC (below it on an uptrend), volume is concentrating at a level the market can't sustain — a sign "the market is kind of getting a little bit weaker." (v273, [07:18]–[09:56])
- **Fresh volume**: Volume at extreme lows/highs at LOD/HOD is "expected to be there" — passive buyers/hedgers have pre-positioned bids. This volume is less actionable. Volume that appears fresh in a formerly balanced area that price is now leaving is more meaningful because it represents new conviction. (v278, [03:36]–[04:09])

## G. Delta / delta-divergence ideas

- **Delta neutral band for e-minis**: "plus or minus 25" — delta within ±25 in e-minis is treated as essentially zero/neutral, no directional weight given. Explicit threshold stated. (v274, [15:51]) [NEW — distinct from ±50 in digest]

- Min Delta vs. Max Delta tracking: monitoring the strongest aggressive selling point (Min Delta) and the strongest aggressive buying point (Max Delta) within a bar intrabar, and their relationship to final delta, as leading evidence of a turn. When Min Delta was -1200 and Final Delta was -700, the 500-contract recovery between Min and Final signals supportive buying even in a bearish-looking bar. (v274, [09:55]–[10:31])

- **Delta pressure** concept: market direction and delta sign can diverge when passive traders absorb aggressive flow. Going up on negative delta = supportive passive buying (bullish). ## R. Contradictions / nuances

This appears to partially qualify the "stopping volume" setup — stopping volume requires the POC to be unusually concentrated at the extreme relative to bar total, not merely that "there is volume" at the low. - **Stacked imbalances on volatile days fire for a different reason**: on normal days, stacked imbalances represent aggressive retail/institutional flow in one direction. On high-impact news days, they represent large orders executed quickly by execution desks regardless of conviction. This means the "aggressor exhaustion" story does not apply in the same way to news-day stacked imbalances — they are execution artifacts, not sentiment signals per se. (v275, [04:51])
- **Fresh volume vs. extreme volume**: volume at LOD/HOD is structurally expected (passive hedgers/end-users). Volume that appears in a formerly balanced area as price is now escaping that area is the higher-quality signal. This creates a hierarchy for stopping-volume interpretation. (v278, [03:36])
