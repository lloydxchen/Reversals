## A. Setup types & names he uses

- **Flow-driven market breakout / third-failed-divergence breakout** (bullish or bearish): Entry on breakout of the high/low that violated a third consecutive failed divergence. The setup is directional — bullish if three bullish divergences fail (buy stop above third level), bearish if three bearish divergences fail (sell stop below third level). [REPEATED] (v199, [30:30]–[37:39])

- **Divergence reversal** (standard, not flow-driven): Single or double delta divergence at a HOD/LOD, expected to reverse price. He distinguishes this from the flow-driven version — standalone divergences can reverse price in "normal" markets; the same signal failing repeatedly is what identifies a flow-driven day. [REPEATED] (v199, [21:25])

- **Trapped-trader reversal**: Price reverses after showing trapped-trader indicator (arrow up = trapped shorts → bullish, arrow down = trapped longs → bearish). Works at swing extremes and also mid-trend at smaller retracements. [REPEATED] (v200, [27:47]–[29:15])

- **EVA (Expanded/Enhanced Value Area) signal**: Bullish or bearish expanded value area highlighted by software on a bar. Provides entry signal with stop just below the low of the EVA bar (bullish) or above the high (bearish). [ONCE] (v200, [38:22]–[42:31])

- **Value area migration read** (bias filter, not a trigger): Consecutive bars showing value area moving higher = bullish bias; moving lower = bearish bias. Prevents fading a trend. [ONCE] (v200, [35:44]–[37:13])

- **Order flow sequencing entry** (bullish/bearish): Market eating through successively larger size on the offer (bullish sequencing) or bid (bearish sequencing) across a sequence of price levels. A software-highlighted condition; don't trade in isolation — put in context. [ONCE] (v200, [47:13]–[53:51])

- **Trapped traders + EVA combined**: Gold and e-mini examples; trap traders + EVA (bearish) = don't buy, anticipate further decline. Trap traders + EVA (bullish) = long signal with tight stop below EVA bar low. [ONCE] (v200, [39:28]–[40:29])

## C. Order-flow story & psychology per setup

- **Flow-driven market / three-failed-divergence**: Short-term technically-driven traders (intraday scalpers) keep entering at HOD/LOD divergences expecting a reversal; long-term traders (banks, hedge funds, commercials) keep absorbing that attempt and dominating flow. The technically-driven buyers "are not getting rewarded for their effort" — market goes sideways or lower despite their activity. After the third failure, those traders are exhausted and wrong-footed; the long-term flow takes over and price runs. (v199, [21:55]–[22:55]) [REPEATED]

- **Trapped traders**: Traders who entered a position that is now offside. Arrow-down = trapped longs (bearish sign); arrow-up = trapped shorts (bullish sign). Key nuance: the trapped quantity itself is **small** and is NOT the fuel for the move. Rather, "it's a sign that there's bigger forces at work in the market because... it's a sign of people's thought of the market going in a direction and they're wrong — the retail aspect of the market is wrong." (v200, [27:47]–[28:17]) [REPEATED]

- **EVA (bullish)**: Expanded value area at the low of a move shows that meaningful volume and acceptance is happening at that level — not just a transient touch. The market has "value" there; buyers are absorbing sellers and price should trade back to or above that value area. Pin bar without this context fails; with it, you know "this low is the low to buy." (v200, [40:29]–[41:28])

- **Order flow sequencing**: Large participants "eating through levels" — aggressive buyers lifting successively bigger offers as they fill their order. The market "had to work harder to get through a level" = it is stronger, not weaker. Sequencing is the footprint of institutional accumulation/distribution in real time. (v200, [47:13]–[52:10])

- **Value area migration down + bearish EVA + trap traders (bearish combo)**: "There's absolutely no reason to be getting long here." Retail buyers keep trying to "buy the dip" while all three signals say institutions are distributing. (v200, [39:28])

## G. Delta / delta-divergence ideas

- **Three-failed-divergence rule for flow-driven market identification**: Two failed divergences seen → watch for third; if third also fails (price breaks through the level that should have reversed), the market is flow-driven. Entry on the breakout of the third. This is a structured, named setup in his framework. (v199, [30:30]–[33:57]) [REPEATED]

- **Divergence types defined clearly**: Bearish divergence = new or equal high with negative delta. Bullish divergence = new or equal low with positive delta. (v199, [26:31]) [REPEATED]

- **Failed divergence as stronger signal than fulfilled divergence**: "What doesn't happen in the market is oftentimes more important than what does happen as expected." A divergence that *should* produce a reversal but doesn't is a stronger confirmation of the underlying trend. (v199, [28:59]–[29:29]) [REPEATED nuance]

- **Divergences in flow-driven markets**: Even after 3 failed divergences the market may continue posting new divergences; he says "don't have to get long immediately" — the stop order placed above/below the level handles entry. (v199, [35:11])

## R. Contradictions / nuances

- **Flow-driven day = don't fade divergences**: In a flow-driven market, the standard divergence reversal setup should be skipped or *reversed* (trade the breakout, not the bounce). This is a conditional inversion of the normal divergence-reversal model. A divergence that would normally be A+ is actually a trap signal on a flow day. (v199, [13:53]–[14:22]) [ONCE — explicit]

- **Trapped trader quantity does NOT fuel the move**: He says explicitly: "I'm not looking at oh you know there's 100 traders that were long and wrong here because you know that's causing this move down — no." The small trapped quantity is a symptom, not a cause. The move is caused by larger forces. (v200, [28:17]) — this clarifies/refines the "trapped traders as squeeze fuel" narrative that some traders hold, and partially reinforces the digest nuance.

- **Divergences: sometimes yes, sometimes no**: "You know divergences... you'd short it. Well sometimes yes, sometimes no." The signal is contextual. Same technology also identifies flow-driven breakouts. (v199, [26:31]–[27:00])

- **Textbook candlestick failed**: He shows a "textbook bullish pin bar" that failed and argues this is not rare — illustrating that visual patterns without order flow context are unreliable even when they "look perfect." Contradicts the idea that certain candlestick shapes alone are A+ reversals. (v200, [40:29])

- **Forex not suitable for order flow**: He explicitly says forex data is not centralized and is therefore unsuitable for order flow analysis. This is a practical limitation not often stated. (v200, [1:12:45]) [ONCE]

- **Order flow sequencing: don't trade every signal in isolation** — must be in context of market. (v200, [53:19]) Same qualification applies to every tool.

- **EVA entry is earlier than breakout**: Buying the EVA bar rather than waiting for the breakout gives earlier and tighter entry. This is a mild contradiction of typical "wait for confirmation" advice, though he qualifies it as requiring the EVA signal. (v200, [43:53])
