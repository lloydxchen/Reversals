## A. Setup types & names he uses
- **Exhaustion print** (formerly "small print") — last buyer bought / last seller sold; very low volume at the edge of a bar after a move. Bullish at swing low / low-of-day; bearish at swing high / high-of-day. (v367, "Market Exhaustion - My Favorite Easiest", [00:36]; v394, "Identify Exhaustion", [04:26]) [REPEATED]
- **Delta weakness / "market weakness"** — sustained sequence of weakening Delta (strong → neutral), at least 3 bars; bullish (negative Delta drying up at a low) or bearish (positive Delta drying up at a high). (v371, "Delta Weakness What It Means", [01:22]; v376, "Delta Weakness And The Importance", [04:33]) [REPEATED]
- **Delta reversal** — compare internal Max/Min Delta to final Delta in one bar; a big intra-bar Delta that reverses back toward zero/opposite (long lower wick on a Delta candle for a buy; long upper wick for a sell). (v375, "Delta Reversals", [03:01]) [ONCE-heavy, single dedicated video]
- **Orderflows ratios** — "ratio bounds high" = price rejection/exhaustion (ratio ≥ 30); "ratio bounds low" = price support/defense (ratio ≤ 0.69, his real value 0.7). Bullish ratio (blue) on green bar; bearish ratio (red) on red bar = potential reversal. (v372, "How To Use Orderflows Ratios", [05:30], [07:39]) [ONCE-heavy]
- **Order flow Divergence** — new/equal low with positive Delta (bullish), pairs with bullish ratio for "bread and butter almost automatic trades." (v372, [10:21]) [ONCE]
- **Imbalances** — stacked imbalances, multiple imbalances (3+ buying or selling in one bar), at swing highs/lows. (v363, "Trading Order Flow Imbalances", [03:50], [05:22]) [REPEATED]
- **Stop run** (vs trapped traders) — stacked selling imbalance + no counter trade at a low after a move = a triggered stop, market snaps back. (v390, "Use Order Flow Volume", [05:47]) [ONCE]
- **Trapped traders** ("stock traders") — long-and-wrong / short-in-the-hole; bought the high or sold the low of a failed breakout. (v390, [00:22], [00:48]) [REPEATED]
- **Delta surge** — three increasing Delta bars (mentioned in passing as the bullish follow-through). (v376, [07:30]) [ONCE]
- **"Three in a row" / "Three Musketeers / Three blind mice"** — 3 bullish or 3 bearish ratios in a row → often a pullback into the 2nd/3rd bar (an old tested system he "moved on from"). (v372, [14:35], [15:04]) [ONCE]

## C. Order-flow story & psychology per setup
- **Exhaustion**: at the high, "the last buyer has bought" → no more buying pressure → market reverses; mirror at the low ("last seller has sold"). Attributed to Tom DeMark seminar quote. (v367, [02:41]; v394, [02:52]) [REPEATED]
- **Local stop-probe game**: locals buy 1 lot at the new high (lift the offer) to "see if there could be any stops up here" and trigger a 3–4 tick run; an exhaustion print of 1–2 lots means no stops were resting there. (v367, [04:06], [04:28]) [ONCE]
- **Delta weakness psychology**: "if that aggressive buying on a move up is weakening, who's going to take the market higher once those aggressive buyers... are done buying? No one. The aggressive selling... takes over." Selling pressure likened to **water pressure** dying out / "spigot turned off." (v371, [03:00]; v376, [05:07]) [REPEATED]
- **Trapped traders reality-check**: ~30 contracts at a gold high is NOT enough to "stop the move and reverse it" — could be traded out in 30 seconds. He pushes back on people who blame big reversals on a handful of trapped day-traders. (v390, [02:12], [02:40]) [REPEATED]
- **Stop vs trapped trader**: a sudden stacked sell imbalance with zero counter-trade at a low after a move = a resting stop being triggered, then market "snaps right back." (v390, [04:45], [05:47]) [ONCE]
- **Big institutional sellers don't panic**: an institution short at crude 91 that rallies 10–25 cents against it treats that as "noise," not heat — so the naive "they're scrambling to cover" trapped-trader story is wrong for big size. (v390, [10:49], [11:16]) [ONCE]
- **Pullback-buy psychology**: aggressive buying on a pullback that "weakens and is basically shut off" lets you get short at low risk because no one is left to push it up. (v371, [05:09]) [ONCE]

## D. Exhaustion clues
- Very low volume at the extreme edge of a bar after a move: e.g. "4 lots traded" at the high vs "2 lots" at the low while reversing. (v367, [03:33]) [REPEATED]
- On up-moves exhaustion prints appear at the **bottom (bid side) of green candles**; on down-moves at the **top (offer side) of red candles**. (v367, [06:39]) [REPEATED]
- Threshold is market-specific: value of **9 in most markets**; can use 25 or 50 on heavy-volume 10-year (each bar 16k–32k contracts); **1 contract** works on thin markets (Euro, British pound, Hogs); 5 used as a mid value. NOT set in stone. (v367, [11:02], [11:36]; v394, [10:33], [11:10], [14:39]) [REPEATED]
- Relative-volume context matters more than the absolute number: 27 traded at the high while other levels traded 200/500/700/2000/5000 still qualifies. (v367, [12:11]) [ONCE]
- "Keep pounding into this low, selling is getting lesser and lesser" then an exhaustion print → rally. (v394, [11:43]) [ONCE]
- Ultra/e-mini: liquid markets "distribute volume fairly well" so exhaustion prints are **rare on highs/lows**, more common mid-move; better used as a hold/stay-in signal. (v367, [08:50], [09:22]) [REPEATED]

## E. Absorption clues
- Implied via the "big seller / big volume that holds its foot on the neck of the market" test: heavy offers (e.g. crude 114/103, 181) — watch whether the supply produces follow-through (more down) or fails (next bar trades above it). Failure to absorb/continue = reversal. (v390, [07:42], [09:21]) [ONCE]
- "When a big order in the market is removed... once it's finally filled" the market is likely to bounce (seller done → no more supply). The 415-contract offer at crude low: once gone, retest shows "no more selling," market rallies. (v390, [14:50], [15:56]) [ONCE]
- NOTE: he does not use the word "absorption" much in this chunk; most absorption-adjacent talk is framed as "is the big seller's foot still on the market." NEEDS-OPERATIONALIZATION (qualitative "enough to keep its foot on the neck").

## F. Stacked imbalance ideas
- Imbalance trigger default **400% = 4:1** (his preference). Industry standard he cites as 4:1; others use 3:1 (300%, more signals), 5:1, 10:1 (1000%), even 100:1 (fewer, only the ones that "really stick out"). (v363, [00:00]; v386, [10:31]) [REPEATED]
- **Minimum imbalance volume threshold = 10 contracts** (so 7-vs-0 is ignored; wants "lines in the sand drawn"). (v386, [09:20], [09:58]) [ONCE]
- Higher % ratio recommended on **more volatile markets** (NQ at 3:1 gives an imbalance nearly every bar → useless; bump to 10:1). Match ratio to volatility. (v363, [13:56], [15:04]) [REPEATED]
- **Stacked imbalance must be given time to retest**: he draws the zone "until tested"; ideally next bar comes back and shows MORE imbalance in the same direction (more selling into a sell zone). (v363, [04:26], [04:54]) [ONCE]
- **Multiple imbalances** = 3+ buying or 3+ selling imbalances in one bar; he hunts them at swing highs/lows. Aggressive selling (multiple selling imbalances) at the high of day = "the market just screaming at me... potentially taking a short." (v363, [05:22], [07:29]) [REPEATED]
- **Avoid conflicting bars**: a bar of multiple/stacked SELLING imbalances immediately followed by a bar of multiple/stacked BUYING imbalances → "I just stay away from those bars completely." (v363, [06:29]) [ONCE]
- Imbalance definition is footprint-quantified buying vs selling, NOT a price-action gap between candle wicks (he explicitly rejects the price-action "imbalance" definition). (v363, [01:46], [02:11]) [REPEATED]
- **Position relative to a stacked imbalance flips its meaning**: trading ABOVE a stacked selling imbalance is bullish ("Uno reverse cards it"); you'd only stay bearish if trading BELOW it. (v390, [06:40], [07:13]) [ONCE]

## G. Delta / delta-divergence ideas
- **Delta = net aggressive buying − aggressive selling in a bar.** One bar's Delta in a vacuum "doesn't have much meaning"; value comes over a sustained sequence. (v371, [00:00], [01:53]) [REPEATED]
- **Delta weakness pattern**: e.g. −609 → −310 → −26 (selling drying up) at/near a low = potential reversal; mirror −976/811/894/516/189 (buying drying up) at a high = bearish. (v371, [01:53], [03:00]) [ONCE-with numbers]
- He even watches **intra-bar Delta decay**: a bar that hit Max Delta +711 but finished +189 = buying weakened within the bar. (v371, [03:00]) [ONCE]
- **Market weakness detector** needs a sequence of **3 or more weakening Deltas** (positive or negative). Can shorten to 2 (more signals, over-trading) but "**three is about just nice**"; a setting of 4 is "quite strong / too strong" especially on 1-min e-mini. (v371, [09:32], [10:06], [12:22]; v376, [08:56]) [REPEATED]
- **Delta reversal** mechanics: compare internal Max/Min Delta to final Delta in ONE bar. Buy = new/lower swing low, strong intra-bar negative Delta that reverses back toward 0/positive (long lower wick). Sell = swing high, strong positive Delta that reverses to ~0/negative (long upper wick, small body). (v375, [03:01], [04:00]) [ONCE-heavy]
- **±50 neutral band** on liquid e-mini: a final Delta within ±50 is treated as neutral/back-to-zero (one extra trade / a nanosecond could flip sign). So a small negative final Delta (e.g. −45) can still count as a delta reversal. (v375, [05:57]) [ONCE]
- **Delta candles** tool visualizes this (wick = max excursion, body = final). He prefers them over parsing numbers ("the mind processes images at a much faster rate"). Equivalent to NinjaTrader's "Order Flow Cumulative Delta" set to **Bar (not Session)**. (v375, [01:58], [21:28]) [REPEATED]
- **Delta divergence**: new/equal low with positive Delta = bullish divergence; can also draw rising Delta trendlines off a swing low while price falls. (v372, [10:21]; v375, [12:13]) [REPEATED]
- **Delta-volume coloring**: when Delta-volume per price lights up **magenta for consecutive bars** (e.g. crude −28/−15/−15/−28...) = sign of strong, consistent selling; final Deltas closing near the bar's Min Delta = strong. Default Delta-volume threshold 25. (v390, [12:58], [13:38]) [ONCE]

## H. Failed breakout / breakdown / stop-run / liquidity-sweep ideas
- **Stop run signature**: stacked selling imbalance with NO counter trade (e.g. gold 52: 52/36/29/22/0/0/0/1 on offer) right at a low after a move = a resting stop triggered, then market "snaps right back." Distinguished from a real big order. (v390, [04:16], [04:45], [05:47]) [ONCE]
- **Stop-probe at extremes**: locals lift the offer with 1 lot at a new high to trip stops; exhaustion print of 1–2 lots = no stops there. (v367, [04:06]) [ONCE]
- **Trapped-trader / failed-breakout debunk**: a big wick or a small failed breakout is NOT proof of mass trapped traders; quantify with volume — usually it's 30-ish contracts, easily absorbed. (v390, [01:16], [02:12]) [REPEATED]
- **Failed example honesty**: many shown setups "failed" first then the next instance worked — he stresses you take the stop and look for the next one (risk-management framing). (v371, [04:09]; v372, [12:28]) [REPEATED]
- **Previous resistance → support (and vice-versa)**: big-volume level acts as S/R on retest; market trades up to old resistance, comes back to that volume level, bounces. (v390, [11:45]) [REPEATED]

## I. Trapped-trader ideas
- Definition: "long and wrong or short in the hole" — sold the low / bought the high of a breakout that never materialized; he calls them "stock traders" (term picked up in Singapore). (v390, [00:22], [00:48]) [REPEATED]
- **Quantify before believing**: count the actual contracts; ~30 at a high or ~140 in a 981-volume bar leaves "plenty of room for them to cover" → not enough to drive a reversal. (v390, [02:12], [05:19]) [ONCE]
- **Time-frame caveat**: trapped-trader thinking assumes everyone is intraday; big/institutional traders hold longer time frames and treat small adverse moves as noise, so they aren't forced to puke. (v390, [10:17], [11:16]) [ONCE]

## K. Confirmation — what SHOULD happen quickly after entry/trigger
- Move should happen **soon, not after going sideways 5–10 minutes**: "you're trying to take advantage of this right away... I'm not looking for it to go sideways for five minutes ten minutes and then sell off." (v363, [09:48]) [REPEATED]
- After exhaustion print at a low → quick rally (examples: e-mini 25-contract print → "nice move up"; pound low of day → rally almost to 14). (v367, [12:47]; v394, [11:43]) [REPEATED]
- In-trade confirmation via exhaustion prints in your direction: if long and seeing exhaustion prints on the **bottom of green candles**, "that's a good sign... market is going in your direction." (v367, [09:22]) [REPEATED]
- Delta should flip and build: "negative → weaker → positive → more positive → more positive" over ~4–6 bars precedes a rally. (v376, [06:54]) [ONCE]
- Stacked imbalance confirmation = **follow-through in the very next bar** (more selling into a sell zone). (v363, [04:54]; v390, [06:14]) [REPEATED]

## L. Invalidation — what should NOT happen / what kills the thesis
- After multiple selling imbalances at a high, if "next bar trades one tick lower and then rallies back inside, that's a bad sign in the order flow" — thesis dead. (v363, [09:48]) [ONCE]
- Exhaustion print invalidated if price trades **below the exhaustion-print level** (for a long): "if we trade below that it's not exhaustion." Stop goes just behind that line. (v394, [06:44], [06:17]) [REPEATED]
- Delta reversal invalidated / lower quality if the **previous bar has a stronger Delta** in the reversing direction (e.g. prior bars −478, −848 stronger than the signal's −392). (v375, [11:46], [16:33]) [REPEATED]
- Signal in the **middle of a range / not reversing anything** = invalid: "what's the point of trading a Delta reversal if it's not reversing anything." (v375, [07:16]) [REPEATED]
- Setup too **late in the day / already started reversing** = skip (delta reversal already coming off the high, after 1 p.m.). (v375, [18:03], [20:07]) [ONCE]
- Stacked selling imbalance that the market is trading ABOVE = NOT a continuation-down thesis. (v390, [07:13]) [ONCE]

## M. Stop / risk / target / trade management
